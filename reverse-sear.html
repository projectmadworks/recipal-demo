Reverse-Sear Steak

The reverse-sear method produces a more evenly cooked steak than traditional high-heat searing alone. By gently bringing a thick bone-in ribeye up to temperature in a low oven first, then finishing it in a smoking-hot cast iron pan, you achieve edge-to-edge doneness with a deep, caramelised crust — and a butter baste with garlic and thyme to finish.

    Instructions
    1. 01
      Salt the steak and bring to room temperature
      Season a 2-inch bone-in ribeye very generously with salt and cracked black pepper on all sides, including the edges. Set it on a wire rack over a baking sheet and leave it uncovered at room temperature for 30 minutes. Salting the meat in advance and allowing it to rest draws moisture to the surface, which then gets reabsorbed — resulting in better moisture retention and a more seasoned interior throughout.
    2. 02
      Slow-roast in a low oven
      Preheat your oven to 250°F (120°C). Place the steak on the rack in the oven and roast until the internal temperature reaches 115°F / 46°C — approximately 45–60 minutes depending on thickness. The steak will look pale and unappetising at this stage. That is expected. The crust comes entirely from the pan.
      🌡 Check the temperature from the side of the steak, inserting the probe into the thickest part away from the bone.
    3. 03
      Heat the cast iron until smoking
      Place a cast iron skillet over high heat for 3–4 minutes until it is visibly smoking. Add the leaf lard or a high-smoke-point neutral oil and swirl to coat. The pan must be genuinely hot before the steak goes in — a pan that is too cool will steam the surface rather than sear it, and the crust will not develop properly.
    4. 04
      Sear and develop the crust
      Carefully place the steak in the centre of the pan. Do not move it. Allow it to sear undisturbed until a deep brown crust has formed on the underside — approximately 1–2 minutes. Because the steak is already close to temperature from the oven, the sear phase is brief and focused entirely on building colour and texture on the exterior.
    5. 05
      Flip and butter-baste
      Flip the steak once. Reduce heat to medium and add a generous knob of unsalted butter, a whole smashed garlic clove, and a sprig of fresh thyme to the pan. Once the butter begins to foam, tilt the pan toward you and use a spoon to continuously baste the melted butter over the top of the steak for 1–2 minutes. This enriches the crust and infuses the meat with the aromatics.
    6. 06
      Rest under a butter blanket
      Transfer the steak to a cutting board. Spoon the remaining butter, garlic clove, and thyme sprig from the pan over the top of the steak. Allow it to rest for a minimum of 10 minutes before carving. Resting allows the internal juices to redistribute — cutting too early will result in a significant loss of moisture.
      ⏱ Set a timer and step away. 10 minutes is the minimum; 12–15 is better for a steak this size.
    7. 07
      Carve and serve
      Run your knife down along the bone to separate the meat, then slice the steak into approximately ½-inch (1.2 cm) pieces against the grain. Fan the slices out on a warm plate. Finish with a pinch of flaky sea salt if desired and serve immediately.

    Cook's Notes

    The reverse-sear is particularly well-suited to thick cuts — 1.5 inches and above. Thinner steaks cook too quickly in the oven phase to benefit from it. The low oven temperature produces a very gradual, even rise in internal temperature, which means there is almost no overcooked grey band around the edge of the steak. The result is consistent doneness from crust to centre. Leaf lard is the traditional fat of choice for cast iron searing due to its high smoke point and neutral flavour, but refined avocado oil or grapeseed oil are practical alternatives. Avoid butter alone for the initial sear — it will burn before the crust develops.
